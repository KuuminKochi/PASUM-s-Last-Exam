Question 1 [6 marks]

Two identical cells, each of e.m.f. $1.5 \, \text{V}$ and internal resistance $r$, are connected to three identical $6 \, \Omega$ resistors $R_1$, $R_2$, and $R_3$ as shown in Figure 1. If the ammeter reads $1.0 \, \text{A}$, determine the magnitude of the: - (a) equivalent resistance of the three resistors - (b) internal resistance $r$

Need a hint?

For (a), the three $6 \, \Omega$ resistors are in parallel. For (b), use the loop equation. Two cells in parallel give effective emf of $1.5 \, \text{V}$ (same as one cell) but lower internal resistance. Apply $V = \varepsilon - Ir_{\text{eq}}$.

Show solution Given:

- Two identical cells, each with $\varepsilon = 1.5 \, \text{V}$, internal resistance $r$ - Three $6 \, \Omega$ resistors in parallel - Ammeter reading: $I = 1.0 \, \text{A}$

(a) Equivalent resistance of three $6 \, \Omega$ resistors in parallel:

For resistors in parallel:

$$\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{6} = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}$$ $$R_{\text{eq}} = 2 \, \Omega$$ (b) Internal resistance $r$:

When two identical voltage sources ($\varepsilon, r$) are in parallel: - Total emf: $\varepsilon_{\text{total}} = 1.5 \, \text{V}$ (same as one cell) - Total internal resistance: $r_{\text{eq}} = r/2$ (two $r$'s in parallel)

Apply the circuit equation:

$$V = \varepsilon_{\text{total}} - Ir_{\text{eq}}$$

The terminal voltage across the external resistors:

$$V = I R_{\text{eq}} = (1.0)(2) = 2 \, \text{V}$$

So:

$$2 = 1.5 - \frac{r}{2}(1.0)$$ $$\frac{r}{2} = 1.5 - 2 = -0.5$$

This gives negative r, which is impossible. Let me reconsider the circuit configuration.

Actually, looking at the circuit again - the two cells might be connected differently. Let me solve using the correct interpretation:

For the full circuit: - Total emf (two cells in series aiding): $\varepsilon_{\text{total}} = 3.0 \, \text{V}$ - Total internal resistance (two cells in series): $r_{\text{total}} = 2r$

The external circuit has three $6 \, \Omega$ resistors in parallel = $2 \, \Omega$

Using $V = \varepsilon - Ir$:

$$IR_{\text{eq}} = \varepsilon_{\text{total}} - I(2r)$$ $$(1.0)(2) = 3.0 - 2r$$ $$2 = 3.0 - 2r$$ $$2r = 1.0$$ $$r = 0.5 \, \Omega$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: When cells are connected in series, their emfs add. Two $1.5 \, \text{V}$ cells in series give $3.0 \, \text{V}$. Internal resistances also add in series ($2r$). However, if cells were in parallel, emf stays at $1.5 \, \text{V}$ but internal resistance becomes $r/2$.

Question 2 [5 marks]

When a cell (non-ideal emf) supplies $0.20 \, \text{A}$, its terminal voltage is found to be $1.44 \, \text{V}$. When the same cell supplies $0.60 \, \text{A}$, its terminal voltage is $1.32 \, \text{V}$. Compute the cell's terminal voltage when it supplies $1.0 \, \text{A}$.

Need a hint?

For a non-ideal cell, terminal voltage $V = \varepsilon - Ir$, where $\varepsilon$ is the emf and $r$ is internal resistance. You have two data points - use them to find $\varepsilon$ and $r$, then calculate V at 1.0A.

Show solution Given:

- When $I_1 = 0.20 \, \text{A}$: $V_1 = 1.44 \, \text{V}$ - When $I_2 = 0.60 \, \text{A}$: $V_2 = 1.32 \, \text{V}$

Step 1: Find emf and internal resistance

The terminal voltage equation: $V = \varepsilon - Ir$

For first condition:

$$1.44 = \varepsilon - 0.20r \quad \text{(1)}$$

For second condition:

$$1.32 = \varepsilon - 0.60r \quad \text{(2)}$$

Subtract (2) from (1):

$$1.44 - 1.32 = -0.20r + 0.60r$$ $$0.12 = 0.40r$$ $$r = 0.30 \, \Omega$$

Substitute back into (1):

$$1.44 = \varepsilon - 0.20(0.30)$$ $$1.44 = \varepsilon - 0.06$$ $$\varepsilon = 1.50 \, \text{V}$$ Step 2: Find terminal voltage at $I = 1.0 \, \text{A}$ $$V = \varepsilon - Ir = 1.50 - 1.0(0.30) = 1.50 - 0.30 = 1.20 \, \text{V}$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: This is a classic problem of determining internal resistance. The emf ($\varepsilon$) is the "ideal" voltage when no current flows (open circuit). Terminal voltage drops linearly as current increases due to internal resistance. The slope of the V-I graph gives $-r$.

Question 4 [6 marks]

This question is based on Figure 4. - (a) Apply Kirchhoff Junction Rule (KJR) at node B and write down the equation for currents $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$. - (b) Apply Kirchhoff Loop Rule (KLR) at Loop 1 (L1) and write down the equation for potential changes through FABEF. - (c) Apply Kirchhoff Loop Rule at Loop 2 (L2) and write down the equation for potential changes through BCDEB.

Need a hint?

For (a): KJR states that sum of currents entering a junction = sum leaving. For (b) and (c): KLR states that sum of potential changes around any closed loop = 0. Be careful with sign conventions - going from - to + through a battery is a voltage rise (+Œµ), going from + to - is a voltage drop (-Œµ). Going through a resistor in direction of current is a voltage drop (-IR).

Show solution Given: Circuit with $6 \, \Omega$, $3 \, \Omega$, $9 \, \Omega$ resistors, $15 \, \text{V}$ and $12 \, \text{V}$ sources. (a) Kirchhoff Junction Rule at node B:

Currents $I_1$ and $I_2$ flow into node B, current $I_3$ flows out:

$$I_1 + I_2 = I_3$$

Or equivalently:

$$I_1 + I_2 - I_3 = 0$$ (b) Kirchhoff Loop Rule for Loop 1 (L1): FABEF

Starting from point F, going through A‚ÜíB‚ÜíE‚ÜíF: - From F to A through $15 \, \text{V}$ source (negative to positive): $+15 \, \text{V}$ - From A to B through $6 \, \Omega$ resistor (in direction of $I_1$): $-6I_1$ - From B to E through $3 \, \Omega$ resistor (in direction of $I_3$): $-3I_3$ - From E to F through $12 \, \text{V}$ source (positive to negative): $-12 \, \text{V}$

Sum around the loop:

$$15 - 6I_1 - 3I_3 - 12 = 0$$ $$3 - 6I_1 - 3I_3 = 0$$ $$6I_1 + 3I_3 = 3$$

Or:

$$2I_1 + I_3 = 1 \quad \text{(Equation L1)}$$ (c) Kirchhoff Loop Rule for Loop 2 (L2): BCDEB

Starting from B, going through C‚ÜíD‚ÜíE‚ÜíB: - From B to C through $3 \, \Omega$ resistor (in direction of $I_2$): $-3I_2$ - From C to D through $9 \, \Omega$ resistor (in direction of $I_2$): $-9I_2$ - From D to E through $12 \, \text{V}$ source (positive to negative): $-12 \, \text{V}$ - From E to B through $3 \, \Omega$ resistor (opposite to $I_3$): $+3I_3$

Sum around the loop:

$$-3I_2 - 9I_2 - 12 + 3I_3 = 0$$ $$-12I_2 + 3I_3 = 12$$

Or:

$$3I_3 - 12I_2 = 12$$ $$I_3 - 4I_2 = 4 \quad \text{(Equation L2)}$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: When applying KLR, choose a consistent direction (clockwise or counterclockwise) for each loop. If you go through a component in the direction of assumed current, the voltage drop is $-IR$. If against the current, it's $+IR$. For batteries, going from negative to positive terminal gives $+\varepsilon$.

Question 5 [6 marks]

Based on Figure 5, determine: - (a) the magnitude for currents $I_1$, $I_2$, and $I_3$ - (b) which current direction is wrongly chosen and state the correct direction

Need a hint?

Use the three equations from Q4: 1. $I_1 + I_2 = I_3$ (Junction rule at B) 2. $2I_1 + I_3 = 1$ (Loop 1) 3. $I_3 - 4I_2 = 4$ (Loop 2)

Solve these three equations simultaneously. If a current comes out negative, the actual direction is opposite to what was assumed.

Show solution Using the equations from Q4:

1. Junction rule at B: $I_1 + I_2 = I_3$ 2. Loop 1: $2I_1 + I_3 = 1$ 3. Loop 2: $I_3 - 4I_2 = 4$

Step 1: Express $I_3$ from equation 1: $$I_3 = I_1 + I_2$$ Step 2: Substitute into equation 2: $$2I_1 + (I_1 + I_2) = 1$$ $$3I_1 + I_2 = 1 \quad \text{(4)}$$ Step 3: Substitute into equation 3: $$(I_1 + I_2) - 4I_2 = 4$$ $$I_1 - 3I_2 = 4 \quad \text{(5)}$$ Step 4: Solve equations (4) and (5):

From (4): $I_2 = 1 - 3I_1$

Substitute into (5):

$$I_1 - 3(1 - 3I_1) = 4$$ $$I_1 - 3 + 9I_1 = 4$$ $$10I_1 = 7$$ $$I_1 = 0.7 \, \text{A}$$

Then:

$$I_2 = 1 - 3(0.7) = 1 - 2.1 = -1.1 \, \text{A}$$

And:

$$I_3 = I_1 + I_2 = 0.7 + (-1.1) = -0.4 \, \text{A}$$ (a) Magnitudes:

- $I_1 = 0.7 \, \text{A}$ - $I_2 = 1.1 \, \text{A}$ - $I_3 = 0.4 \, \text{A}$

(b) Wrong directions:

- $I_2$ is negative: actual direction is from B to C (opposite to assumed) - $I_3$ is negative: actual direction is from E to B (opposite to assumed)

Correct directions: - $I_1$: From F to A (as assumed) = $0.7 \, \text{A}$ - $I_2$: From C to B (opposite to assumed) = $1.1 \, \text{A}$ - $I_3$: From E to B (opposite to assumed) = $0.4 \, \text{A}$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: A negative current in the solution simply means the actual current flows in the opposite direction to what was initially assumed. This is perfectly fine - the magnitude tells you how strong the current is, and the sign tells you the direction. This often happens when you have multiple voltage sources and the "smaller" one actually gets charged by the "larger" one.

Common Patterns

Resistor Combinations:

- Series: $R_{\text{eq}} = R_1 + R_2 + R_3$ - Parallel: $\frac{1}{R_{\text{eq}}} = \frac{1}{R_1} + \frac{1}{R_2} + \frac{1}{R_3}$

EMF and Terminal Voltage:

- $V = \varepsilon - Ir$ (for a cell with internal resistance) - EMF ($\varepsilon$) is the open-circuit voltage

Kirchhoff's Laws:

- Junction Rule: $\sum I_{\text{in}} = \sum I_{\text{out}}$ - Loop Rule: $\sum V = 0$ around any closed loop

Sign Conventions for KLR:

- Through resistor in direction of current: $\Delta V = -IR$ - Through resistor against current: $\Delta V = +IR$ - Through battery from negative to positive: $\Delta V = +\varepsilon$ - Through battery from positive to negative: $\Delta V = -\varepsilon$

Solving Circuit Problems:

1. Label currents $I_1, I_2, I_3$ with arbitrary directions 2. Apply Junction Rule at each junction 3. Apply Loop Rule for independent loops 4. Solve the simultaneous equations 5. Negative current means actual direction is opposite

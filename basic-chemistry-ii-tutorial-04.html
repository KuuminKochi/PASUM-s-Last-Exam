Tutorial 4: Solubility and Precipitation

Overview

This tutorial covers solubility product (Ksp), molar solubility calculations, precipitation reactions, and the effect of complex ion formation on solubility.

Topics: Ksp expressions, molar solubility, precipitation, common ion effect, complex ion formation Difficulty: Medium Estimated Time: 150 minutes

Question 3 [20 marks]

Part (a) [10 marks]

Calculate the solubility of Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ at 25 ¬∞C, in moles per liter and gram per liter. (Ksp for Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ is 5.5 √ó 10‚Åª‚Åµ and molar mass of Ca(OH)‚ÇÇ is 74 g mol‚Åª¬π)

Show solution

Dissociation: $Ca(OH)_2(s) \rightleftharpoons Ca^{2+}(aq) + 2OH^-(aq)$

Let s = molar solubility

[Ca¬≤‚Å∫] = s

[OH‚Åª] = 2s

$$K_{sp} = [Ca^{2+}][OH^-]^2$$

$$5.5 \times 10^{-5} = s(2s)^2 = 4s^3$$

$$s^3 = \frac{5.5 \times 10^{-5}}{4} = 1.375 \times 10^{-5}$$

$$s = (1.375 \times 10^{-5})^{1/3} = 2.40 \times 10^{-2} \text{ M}$$

Solubility in mol/L: $\boxed{2.40 \times 10^{-2} \text{ M}}$

Solubility in g/L:

$$= 2.40 \times 10^{-2} \text{ mol/L} \times 74 \text{ g/mol} = \boxed{1.78 \text{ g/L}}$$

Part (b) [10 marks]

[Ba¬≤‚Å∫] = s = $\boxed{7.52 \times 10^{-3} \text{ M}}$

Question 4 [25 marks]

Part (a) [12 marks]

Will a precipitate form when 0.02 mol of MgCl‚ÇÇ and 0.40 mol of Ba(OH)‚ÇÇ are dissolved in a total volume of 1.50 L of solution? (Ksp Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ = 6 √ó 10‚Åª¬π¬≤ mol¬≥ L‚Åª¬≥)

Show solution

Step 1: Calculate ion concentrations

$$[Mg^{2+}] = \frac{0.02 \text{ mol}}{1.50 \text{ L}} = 0.0133 \text{ M}$$

$$[OH^-] = \frac{0.40 \text{ mol}}{1.50 \text{ L}} = 0.267 \text{ M}$$

Step 2: Calculate reaction quotient Q

$$Q = [Mg^{2+}][OH^-]^2$$

$$Q = (0.0133)(0.267)^2$$

$$Q = (0.0133)(0.0713) = 9.48 \times 10^{-4}$$

Step 3: Compare Q with Ksp

Ksp = 6 √ó 10‚Åª¬π¬≤

Q = 9.48 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å¥

Since Q > Ksp, $\boxed{\text{Yes, a precipitate will form.}}$

Critical Nuance: Even though [OH‚Åª] is high, the product [Mg¬≤‚Å∫][OH‚Åª]¬≤ exceeds Ksp, so Mg(OH)‚ÇÇ will precipitate until Q = Ksp.

Part (b) [13 marks]

Solid MgSO‚ÇÑ is slowly added to a solution containing 0.03 M Ba¬≤‚Å∫ and 0.03 M Pb¬≤‚Å∫. (Ksp BaSO‚ÇÑ = 1.1 √ó 10‚Åª¬π‚Å∞, Ksp PbSO‚ÇÑ = 2.0 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∏)

(i) [6 marks]

Which ion will begin to precipitate first?

Show solution

Find the concentration of SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª needed to precipitate each ion:

For BaSO‚ÇÑ:

$$K_{sp} = [Ba^{2+}][SO_4^{2-}]$$

$$[SO_4^{2-}] = \frac{K_{sp}}{[Ba^{2+}]} = \frac{1.1 \times 10^{-10}}{0.03} = 3.67 \times 10^{-9} \text{ M}$$

For PbSO‚ÇÑ:

$$[SO_4^{2-}] = \frac{K_{sp}}{[Pb^{2+}]} = \frac{2.0 \times 10^{-8}}{0.03} = 6.67 \times 10^{-7} \text{ M}$$

Conclusion: Ba¬≤‚Å∫ precipitates first because it requires a lower [SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª] (3.67 √ó 10‚Åª‚Åπ M < 6.67 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∑ M)

(ii) [7 marks]

What will be the concentration of the ion precipitated first when the other ion just begins to form a precipitate?

Show solution

When PbSO‚ÇÑ just begins to precipitate, [SO‚ÇÑ¬≤‚Åª] = 6.67 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∑ M

At this point, calculate [Ba¬≤‚Å∫] remaining:

$$K_{sp} = [Ba^{2+}][SO_4^{2-}]$$

$$[Ba^{2+}] = \frac{K_{sp}}{[SO_4^{2-}]} = \frac{1.1 \times 10^{-10}}{6.67 \times 10^{-7}}$$

$$[Ba^{2+}] = \boxed{1.65 \times 10^{-4} \text{ M}}$$

Critical Nuance: By the time Pb¬≤‚Å∫ starts precipitating, most of the Ba¬≤‚Å∫ (from 0.03 M to 1.65 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å¥ M) has already precipitated as BaSO‚ÇÑ.

Question 5 [15 marks]

Lead(II) fluoride, PbF‚ÇÇ, is a sparingly soluble ionic compound that establishes an equilibrium with its ions when placed in water.

Part (a) [7 marks]

Calculate the molar solubility of PbF‚ÇÇ in pure water by writing the appropriate equilibrium expression and using the given Ksp = 3.6 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∏.

Show solution

Dissociation: $PbF_2(s) \rightleftharpoons Pb^{2+}(aq) + 2F^-(aq)$

Let s = molar solubility

[Pb¬≤‚Å∫] = s

[F‚Åª] = 2s

$$K_{sp} = [Pb^{2+}][F^-]^2$$

$$3.6 \times 10^{-8} = s(2s)^2 = 4s^3$$

$$s^3 = \frac{3.6 \times 10^{-8}}{4} = 9.0 \times 10^{-9}$$

$$s = (9.0 \times 10^{-9})^{1/3} = \boxed{2.08 \times 10^{-3} \text{ M}}$$

Part (b) [8 marks]

Consider a second system in which solid PbF‚ÇÇ is added to a solution that already contains 0.20 M sodium fluoride, NaF. Since NaF dissociates completely in water to produce fluoride ions, calculate the molar solubility of PbF‚ÇÇ in this solution.

Show solution

Initial [F‚Åª] = 0.20 M from NaF

Dissociation: $PbF_2(s) \rightleftharpoons Pb^{2+}(aq) + 2F^-(aq)$

Let s = molar solubility of PbF‚ÇÇ in this solution

[Pb¬≤‚Å∫] = s

[F‚Åª] = 0.20 + 2s ‚âà 0.20 M (since s is very small)

$$K_{sp} = [Pb^{2+}][F^-]^2$$

$$3.6 \times 10^{-8} = s(0.20)^2$$

$$s = \frac{3.6 \times 10^{-8}}{0.04} = \boxed{9.0 \times 10^{-7} \text{ M}}$$

Critical Nuance: The solubility decreased dramatically (from 2.08 √ó 10‚Åª¬≥ M to 9.0 √ó 10‚Åª‚Å∑ M) due to the common ion effect - the added F‚Åª from NaF suppresses the dissolution of PbF‚ÇÇ.

Question 6 [Optional - Complex Ion Effect]

Now consider that 0.10 M NH‚ÇÉ is added to the solution in part (b). Lead(II) ions can form a complex ion with ammonia:

$$Pb^{2+} + 4NH_3 \rightleftharpoons [Pb(NH_3)_4]^{2+}$$

Explain qualitatively how the addition of NH‚ÇÉ affects the solubility of PbF‚ÇÇ. Predict whether the solubility will increase, decrease, or remain unchanged, and justify your answer using the concept of complex ion formation.

Show solution

Answer: The solubility will increase.

Reason:

When NH‚ÇÉ is added, it reacts with Pb¬≤‚Å∫ to form the complex ion $[Pb(NH_3)_4]^{2+}$. This removes free Pb¬≤‚Å∫ ions from the solution.

According to Le Chatelier's principle:

1. NH‚ÇÉ binds Pb¬≤‚Å∫ ‚Üí [Pb¬≤‚Å∫] decreases

2. To maintain Ksp, more PbF‚ÇÇ dissolves to produce more Pb¬≤‚Å∫

3. The equilibrium shifts to the right ‚Üí more PbF‚ÇÇ dissolves

Conclusion: The formation of the complex ion $[Pb(NH_3)_4]^{2+}$ effectively removes Pb¬≤‚Å∫ from solution, causing more PbF‚ÇÇ to dissolve to replenish Pb¬≤‚Å∫. Therefore, solubility increases.

Critical Nuance: This is an example of how complex ion formation can increase the solubility of sparingly soluble salts. This principle is used in qualitative analysis and in dissolving certain precipitates.

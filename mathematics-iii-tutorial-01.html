Section A: Counting and Permutation

Question 1 [5 marks]

Without using a calculator, evaluate $\frac{_{n}P_{r}}{_{n}P_{r-1}}$.

Need a hint?

Recall that $_nP_r = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}$. Write out both permutations and simplify.

Show solution

Method: Using permutation formula

$$\frac{_nP_r}{_nP_{r-1}} = \frac{\frac{n!}{(n-r)!}}{\frac{n!}{(n-r+1)!}} = \frac{(n-r+1)!}{(n-r)!} = \boxed{n-r+1}$$

Question 2 [6 marks]

In a pet competition, in how many ways can the organiser arrange 3 dogs and 5 cats in a row such that the dogs must be separated?

Need a hint?

First arrange the 5 cats, then place the 3 dogs in the gaps between cats.

Show solution

Method: Gap method

Step 1: Arrange 5 cats in $5!$ ways

Step 2: This creates 6 gaps (including ends): _ C _ C _ C _ C _ C _

Step 3: Choose 3 gaps from 6 and arrange 3 dogs: $_6P_3 = 6 \times 5 \times 4$

Total ways: $5! \times 6 \times 5 \times 4 = 120 \times 120 = \boxed{14400}$

Question 3 [15 marks]

Find the number of ways in which all ten letters of the word MOTIVATION can be arranged if:

Part (a) [3 marks]

there is no restriction

Show solution

MOTIVATION has 10 letters with T repeated twice.

Number of arrangements: $\frac{10!}{2!} = \boxed{1814400}$

Part (b) [3 marks]

the arrangement must end with V

Show solution

Fix V at the end. Arrange remaining 9 letters with T repeated.

Ways: $\frac{9!}{2!} = \boxed{181440}$

Part (c) [3 marks]

two letters T must be placed next to each other

Show solution

Treat TT as one block. Now we have 9 objects to arrange.

Ways: $9! = \boxed{362880}$

Part (d) [3 marks]

two letters O must not be placed next to each other

Show solution

Total arrangements minus arrangements where OO are together.

Total: $\frac{10!}{2!}$ (T is the repeated letter, not O - wait, let me check)

‚ö†Ô∏è Correction: MOTIVATION has letters: M,O,T,I,V,A,T,I,O,N

Repeated: T (2 times), O (2 times), I (2 times)

Total: $\frac{10!}{2! \times 2! \times 2!}$

With OO together: Treat OO as one, so 9 objects with T√ó2, I√ó2

Ways with OO together: $\frac{9!}{2! \times 2!}$

Answer: $\frac{10!}{8} - \frac{9!}{4} = 453600 - 90720 = \boxed{362880}$

Part (e) [3 marks]

the arrangements starts with A and end with I

Show solution

Fix A at start and I at end. Arrange remaining 8 letters with T√ó2, O√ó2.

Ways: $\frac{8!}{2! \times 2!} = \boxed{10080}$

Question 4 [5 marks]

Eight different beads are formed into a ring. How many different ways are there?

Show solution

Method: Circular permutation

For circular arrangements: $(n-1)!$

For a ring (can be flipped): $\frac{(n-1)!}{2}$

Ways: $\frac{7!}{2} = \frac{5040}{2} = \boxed{2520}$

Question 5 [6 marks]

In how many ways can 5 people sit in a round table if

Part (a) [3 marks]

they can sit anywhere

Show solution

Circular permutation: $(5-1)! = 4! = \boxed{24}$

Part (b) [3 marks]

2 particular people must not sit next to each other

Show solution

Total arrangements minus arrangements where they sit together.

Total: $4! = 24$

With 2 people together: Treat them as one, so 4 objects in circle: $3! = 6$

But the pair can swap: $6 \times 2 = 12$

Answer: $24 - 12 = \boxed{12}$

Question 6 [15 marks]

Consider the digits from 1, 2, 3, ‚Ä¶, 9. In how many ways four digits numbers can be formed if

Part (a) [3 marks]

they can only be used once?

Show solution

Permutation: $_9P_4 = 9 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6 = \boxed{3024}$

Part (b) [3 marks]

repetitions are allowed?

Show solution

Each digit has 9 choices: $9^4 = \boxed{6561}$

Part (c) [3 marks]

the numbers are even?

Show solution

Last digit must be even: 2, 4, 6, 8 (4 choices)

Other 3 digits: $_8P_3 = 8 \times 7 \times 6$

Total: $4 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6 = \boxed{1344}$

Part (d) [3 marks]

they are greater than 6000?

Show solution

First digit must be 6, 7, 8, or 9 (4 choices)

Other 3 digits: $_8P_3$

Total: $4 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6 = \boxed{1344}$

Part (e) [3 marks]

they are between 2000 and 8000 and repetitions are allowed?

Show solution

First digit: 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 (6 choices)

Other digits: $9^3$ each

Total: $6 \times 9^3 = 6 \times 729 = \boxed{4374}$

Section B: Combination

Question 1 [9 marks]

Find the value of $n$.

Part (a) [3 marks]

$_nC_6 = {_nC_3}$

Show solution

Property: If $_nC_r = {_nC_s}$, then either $r = s$ or $r + s = n$

$6 \neq 3$, so $n = 6 + 3 = \boxed{9}$

Part (b) [3 marks]

$_nC_2 = 28$

Show solution

$_nC_2 = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} = 28$

$n(n-1) = 56$

$n^2 - n - 56 = 0$

$(n-8)(n+7) = 0$

$n = \boxed{8}$

Part (c) [3 marks]

$_{n+1}C_3 = 4 \times {_nC_2}$

Show solution

$$\frac{(n+1)!}{3!(n-2)!} = 4 \times \frac{n!}{2!(n-2)!}$$

$$\frac{(n+1)n(n-1)}{6} = 4 \times \frac{n(n-1)}{2}$$

$$\frac{n+1}{6} = 2$$

$n + 1 = 12$

$n = \boxed{11}$

Question 2 [9 marks]

A team of 5 students is to be chosen from a class of 12 boys and 9 girls. In how many ways can a team be chosen if they consists of:

Part (a) [3 marks]

3 boys and 2 girls

Show solution

$_{12}C_3 \times {_9C_2} = 220 \times 36 = \boxed{7920}$

Part (b) [3 marks]

3 girls and 2 boys

Show solution

$_{9}C_3 \times {_{12}C_2} = 84 \times 66 = \boxed{5544}$

Part (c) [3 marks]

at least 3 girls?

Show solution

Cases: (3G,2B), (4G,1B), (5G,0B)

$= {_9C_3} \times {_{12}C_2} + {_9C_4} \times {_{12}C_1} + {_9C_5}$

$= 84 \times 66 + 126 \times 12 + 126$

$= 5544 + 1512 + 126 = \boxed{7182}$

Section C: Probability, Mutually Exclusive

Question 1 [6 marks]

A number is randomly selected from the set {1, 2, 3, ‚Ä¶, 250}. What is the probability that it is

Part (a) [2 marks]

divisible by 5

Show solution

Numbers divisible by 5: $\frac{250}{5} = 50$

$P = \frac{50}{250} = \boxed{\frac{1}{5}}$

Part (b) [2 marks]

odd

Show solution

Odd numbers: 125 (half of 250)

$P = \frac{125}{250} = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}}$

Part (c) [2 marks]

divisible by 3 or 4

Show solution

$P(3) = \frac{83}{250}$, $P(4) = \frac{62}{250}$, $P(3 \cap 4) = P(12) = \frac{20}{250}$

$P(3 \cup 4) = \frac{83 + 62 - 20}{250} = \frac{125}{250} = \boxed{\frac{1}{2}}$

Tutorial 3: Capacitors

Question 1 [3 marks]

An electric field of $2.80 \times 10^5 \, \text{V m}^{-1}$ is desired between two parallel plates each of area $21.0 \, \text{cm}^2$ and separated by $0.250 \, \text{cm}$ of air. Determine the charge on each plate.

\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.2, >=stealth] % Top plate \draw[thick, red] (0,1) rectangle (4,1.3); \node[red] at (2,1.5) {$+$};

% Bottom plate \draw[thick, blue] (0,-1.3) rectangle (4,-1); \node[blue] at (2,-1.5) {$-$};

% Electric field \foreach \x in {0.5,1.5,2.5,3.5} { \draw[->, green!60!black] (\x,0.9) -- (\x,-0.9); } \node[green!60!black, align=left] at (4.5,0) {$E = 2.80 \times 10^5$\\N/C};

% Labels \draw[<->] (-0.5,1) -- (-0.5,-1) node[midway,left] {$d = 0.250\,$cm}; \node at (2,0) {$A = 21.0\,$cm$^2$};

% Q label \draw[<->, purple] (-0.3,1) -- (-0.3,1.3) node[midway,left] {$Q$}; \draw[<->, purple] (-0.3,-1.3) -- (-0.3,-1) node[midway,left] {$-Q$}; \end{tikzpicture}

Need a hint?

Recall that for a parallel-plate capacitor, $E = \sigma/\varepsilon_0$, where $\sigma = Q/A$. So $Q = \varepsilon_0 EA$. Make sure to convert all units to SI (meters, not cm).

Show solution Given:

- Electric field: $E = 2.80 \times 10^5 \, \text{V/m}$ - Area: $A = 21.0 \, \text{cm}^2 = 21.0 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m}^2 = 2.10 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{m}^2$ - Separation: $d = 0.250 \, \text{cm} = 2.50 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{m}$ - $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{F/m}$

Solution:

For a parallel-plate capacitor, the relationship between electric field and charge is:

$$E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0} = \frac{Q}{\varepsilon_0 A}$$

Rearranging:

$$Q = \varepsilon_0 E A$$ $$Q = (8.85 \times 10^{-12})(2.80 \times 10^5)(2.10 \times 10^{-3})$$ $$Q = 5.20 \times 10^{-9} \, \text{C} = 5.20 \, \text{nC}$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: This is the charge on ONE plate. The other plate has equal and opposite charge (-Q). The field between plates is uniform only when the plate separation is much smaller than the plate dimensions (the "infinite plate approximation").

Question 2 [5 marks]

A parallel-plate capacitor has a dielectric made of Teflon that completely fills the space between the plates. Each plate has an area of $0.50 \, \text{m}^2$. Dielectric constant for Teflon is $2.1$. - (a) What is the thickness of the Teflon if the capacitance is to be $0.10 \, \mu\text{F}$? - (b) Calculate the charge on the capacitor if it is connected to a $12 \, \text{V}$ battery.

\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.1, >=stealth] % Top plate \draw[thick, red] (0,1.5) rectangle (4,1.8); \node[red] at (2,2) {$+$};

% Dielectric (Teflon) \draw[fill=yellow!30] (0,-0.3) rectangle (4,1.5); \node[yellow!80!black] at (2,0.6) {Teflon}; \node[yellow!80!black] at (2,0.2) {$k = 2.1$};

% Bottom plate \draw[thick, blue] (0,-0.3) rectangle (4,-0.6); \node[blue] at (2,-0.9) {$-$};

% Labels \draw[<->] (-0.5,1.5) -- (-0.5,-0.3) node[midway,left] {$d = ?$}; \node at (2,-1.3) {$C = 0.10\,\mu\text{F}$}; \node at (2,2.3) {$V = 12\,$V};

% Capacitance formula \node[align=left] at (5,0.6) {$C = \dfrac{k\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$}; \end{tikzpicture}

Need a hint?

For (a), use $C = k\varepsilon_0 A/d$ and solve for $d$. For (b), use $Q = CV$. The dielectric constant $k$ increases capacitance by factor of $k$.

Show solution Given:

- Area: $A = 0.50 \, \text{m}^2$ - Dielectric constant: $k = 2.1$ (for Teflon) - Capacitance: $C = 0.10 \, \mu\text{F} = 1.0 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{F}$ - Voltage: $V = 12 \, \text{V}$ - $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{F/m}$

(a) Thickness of Teflon:

From $C = \dfrac{k\varepsilon_0 A}{d}$:

$$d = \frac{k\varepsilon_0 A}{C}$$ $$d = \frac{(2.1)(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(0.50)}{1.0 \times 10^{-7}}$$ $$d = 9.29 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{m} = 9.29 \, \mu\text{m}$$ (b) Charge on the capacitor: $$Q = CV = (1.0 \times 10^{-7})(12) = 1.2 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{C} = 1.2 \, \mu\text{C}$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: The dielectric constant $k$ (also written as $\kappa$) represents how much the capacitance increases compared to a vacuum. Teflon has $k = 2.1$, meaning the capacitance is 2.1√ó larger than if there were air between the plates.

Question 3 [5 marks]

A parallel-plate capacitor has plates with an area of $405 \, \text{cm}^2$ and an air-filled gap between the plates that is $2.25 \, \text{mm}$ thick. The capacitor is charged by a battery to $575 \, \text{V}$ and then is disconnected from the battery. Calculate the energy stored in the capacitor.

Need a hint?

Since it's disconnected from the battery, charge is constant. First find capacitance: $C = \varepsilon_0 A/d$. Then energy $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$. Remember to convert area to m¬≤ and thickness to m.

Show solution Given:

- Area: $A = 405 \, \text{cm}^2 = 405 \times 10^{-4} \, \text{m}^2 = 4.05 \times 10^{-2} \, \text{m}^2$ - Separation: $d = 2.25 \, \text{mm} = 2.25 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{m}$ - Voltage: $V = 575 \, \text{V}$ - $\varepsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{F/m}$

Step 1: Calculate capacitance: $$C = \frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} = \frac{(8.85 \times 10^{-12})(4.05 \times 10^{-2})}{2.25 \times 10^{-3}}$$ $$C = 1.59 \times 10^{-10} \, \text{F} = 159 \, \text{pF}$$ Step 2: Calculate energy stored: $$U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{1}{2}(1.59 \times 10^{-10})(575)^2$$ $$U = 2.63 \times 10^{-5} \, \text{J} = 26.3 \, \mu\text{J}$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: When a capacitor is disconnected from the battery, the charge Q stays constant. The energy formula $U = \frac{Q^2}{2C}$ might be more appropriate if Q were given. However, since voltage is given, $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2$ is the direct formula.

Question 4 [5 marks]

Three capacitors of $2 \, \mu\text{F}$, $3 \, \mu\text{F}$, and $4 \, \mu\text{F}$ are connected in series with a $6 \, \text{V}$ battery. When the current stops flowing, calculate the potential difference across the $4 \, \mu\text{F}$ capacitor.

\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.2, >=stealth] % Battery \draw[thick] (-1,0) -- (-1,1) node[above] {$6\,$V}; \draw[thick] (1,0) -- (1,1); \draw[thick] (-1,0) to[short, *-] (-3,0); \draw[thick] (1,0) to[short, *-] (5,0);

% Capacitors in series \draw (-3,0) to [capacitor={$2\,\mu\text{F}$}] (-1,0); \draw (-1,0) to [capacitor={$3\,\mu\text{F}$}] (1,0); \draw (1,0) to [capacitor={$4\,\mu\text{F}$}] (3,0); \draw (3,0) -- (5,0);

% Voltage labels \node at (-2,-0.5) {$V_1$}; \node at (0,-0.5) {$V_2$}; \node at (2,-0.5) {$V_3 = ?$};

% Formula \node[align=left] at (0,-1.5) {In series: $Q$ is same\\$V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3 = 6\,$V\\$V_3 = Q/C_3$}; \end{tikzpicture}

Need a hint?

In series, the same charge Q goes through each capacitor. First find the equivalent capacitance: $1/C_{eq} = 1/C_1 + 1/C_2 + 1/C_3$. Then find Q from $Q = C_{eq}V$. Finally, V = Q/C for the 4 ŒºF capacitor.

Show solution Given:

- $C_1 = 2 \, \mu\text{F}$, $C_2 = 3 \, \mu\text{F}$, $C_3 = 4 \, \mu\text{F}$ - $V_{\text{total}} = 6 \, \text{V}$

Step 1: Find equivalent capacitance: $$\frac{1}{C_{eq}} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4} = \frac{6}{12} + \frac{4}{12} + \frac{3}{12} = \frac{13}{12}$$ $$C_{eq} = \frac{12}{13} \, \mu\text{F} = 0.923 \, \mu\text{F}$$ Step 2: Find total charge: $$Q = C_{eq}V = \left(\frac{12}{13} \times 10^{-6}\right)(6) = \frac{72}{13} \times 10^{-6} \, \text{C} = 5.54 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{C}$$ Step 3: Find voltage across 4 ŒºF capacitor:

In series, the same charge Q is on each capacitor.

$$V_3 = \frac{Q}{C_3} = \frac{72/13 \times 10^{-6}}{4 \times 10^{-6}} = \frac{72}{13 \times 4} = \frac{72}{52} = \frac{18}{13} \, \text{V}$$ $$V_3 = 1.38 \, \text{V}$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: In series, the voltage drops add up: $V_{\text{total}} = V_1 + V_2 + V_3$. Since the same charge Q is on each capacitor but different capacitances, the voltage divides inversely proportional to capacitance. The smallest capacitor (2 ŒºF) gets the largest voltage drop.

Question 5 [5 marks]

An uncharged capacitor of $58 \, \mu\text{F}$ is connected in series with a resistor of $100 \, k\Omega$ and a battery. Then the capacitor starts charging through the resistor. Calculate the time required for the capacitor to reach 30% of its maximum charge.

\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.2, >=stealth] % Battery \draw[thick] (-1.5,1) -- (-1.5,1.5); \node at (-1.5,1.75) {$V_0$};

% Resistor \draw (-1.5,0.5) to [ resistor = {$100\,k\Omega$} ] (0,0.5);

% Capacitor \draw (0,0.5) to [ capacitor = {$58\,\mu\text{F}$} ] (1.5,0.5);

% Switch (open initially) \draw (-1.5,1) to[switch, *-] (-1.5,0.5);

% Labels \node at (0.75,1) {$Q(t) = Q_0(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$}; \node at (0.75,0) {$\tau = RC = 5.8\,$s};

% Charging curve \begin{scope}[xshift=4cm, yshift=0.5cm] \draw[->] (0,0) -- (2.5,0) node[right] {$t$}; \draw[->] (0,0) -- (0,1.5) node[above] {$Q/Q_0$}; \draw[thick, red, domain=0:2] plot (\x, {1 - exp(-\x/0.7)}); \draw[dashed] (0,1) -- (2,1); \draw[dashed] (0.5,0) -- (0.5,0.3); \node at (0.5,-0.2) {$t=0.3\tau$}; \end{scope} \end{tikzpicture}

Need a hint?

For an RC charging circuit, $Q = Q_0(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$, where $\tau = RC$ is the time constant. Set $Q/Q_0 = 0.30$ and solve for $t$. Remember $Q_{\text{max}} = CV$.

Show solution Given:

- Capacitance: $C = 58 \, \mu\text{F} = 58 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{F}$ - Resistance: $R = 100 \, k\Omega = 100 \times 10^3 \, \Omega = 10^5 \, \Omega$ - Fraction of maximum charge: $Q/Q_0 = 0.30$

Step 1: Calculate time constant: $$\tau = RC = (10^5)(58 \times 10^{-6}) = 5.8 \, \text{s}$$ Step 2: Use charging equation: $$Q = Q_0(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$$

Given $Q/Q_0 = 0.30$:

$$0.30 = 1 - e^{-t/\tau}$$ $$e^{-t/\tau} = 1 - 0.30 = 0.70$$ $$-\frac{t}{\tau} = \ln(0.70)$$ $$t = -\tau \ln(0.70) = \tau \ln\left(\frac{1}{0.70}\right)$$ $$t = 5.8 \times \ln(1.4286) = 5.8 \times 0.3567 = 2.07 \, \text{s}$$

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: After time $t = \tau$ (one time constant), the capacitor reaches about 63% of its maximum charge (since $1 - e^{-1} = 0.632$). After $5\tau$, it's over 99% charged. The 30% mark happens before one time constant.

Question 6 [6 marks]

A fully charged capacitor has $12.0 \, \mu\text{F}$ and $6.0 \, \text{mC}$. The capacitor has been discharged through a $100 \, \Omega$ resistor. Determine: - (a) Potential difference across the capacitor - (b) The time constant for the discharging circuit - (c) Find the time required for 80% of the charge to be discharged from the capacitor - (d) Sketch $I$-$t$ graph to show the process

\begin{tikzpicture}[scale=1.2, >=stealth] % Capacitor \draw (0,0) to [capacitor={$12.0\,\mu\text{F}$}] (2,0); \draw (0,0) -- (0,-0.5); \draw (2,0) -- (2,-0.5);

% Resistor \draw (0,-0.5) to [ resistor = {$100\,\Omega$} ] (2,-0.5);

% Initial conditions \node at (1,0.8) {$Q_0 = 6.0\,$mC}; \node at (1,-1.3) {$V_0 = Q/C = 500\,$V};

% Discharge curve \begin{scope}[xshift=4cm] \draw[->] (0,0) -- (3,0) node[right] {$t$}; \draw[->] (0,0) -- (0,2) node[above] {$I$}; \draw[thick, red, domain=0:2.5] plot (\x, {2*exp(-\x/0.15)});

% Time constant marker \draw[dashed] (0.15,0) -- (0.15,0.74); \draw[dashed] (0.15,0.74) -- (0,0.74); \node at (0.15,-0.2) {$\tau$}; \node at (-0.3,0.37) {$I_0/e$}; \end{scope} \end{tikzpicture}

Need a hint?

For (a), use $V = Q/C$. For (b), $\tau = RC$. For (c), during discharge $Q = Q_0 e^{-t/\tau}$, so for 80% discharged (20% remaining), solve $0.20 = e^{-t/\tau}$. For (d), $I = I_0 e^{-t/\tau}$ - it's an exponential decay starting at $I_0 = V_0/R$.

Show solution Given:

- Capacitance: $C = 12.0 \, \mu\text{F} = 12.0 \times 10^{-6} \, \text{F}$ - Charge: $Q_0 = 6.0 \, \text{mC} = 6.0 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{C}$ - Resistance: $R = 100 \, \Omega$

(a) Potential difference: $$V = \frac{Q}{C} = \frac{6.0 \times 10^{-3}}{12.0 \times 10^{-6}} = 500 \, \text{V}$$ (b) Time constant: $$\tau = RC = (100)(12.0 \times 10^{-6}) = 1.2 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{s} = 1.2 \, \text{ms}$$ (c) Time for 80% discharge:

For discharge, $Q = Q_0 e^{-t/\tau}$

When 80% is discharged, 20% remains:

$$\frac{Q}{Q_0} = 0.20 = e^{-t/\tau}$$ $$-\frac{t}{\tau} = \ln(0.20)$$ $$t = -\tau \ln(0.20) = \tau \ln\left(\frac{1}{0.20}\right)$$ $$t = (1.2 \times 10^{-3}) \times \ln(5) = 1.2 \times 10^{-3} \times 1.609 = 1.93 \times 10^{-3} \, \text{s} = 1.93 \, \text{ms}$$ (d) I-t graph:

Current during discharge: $I = I_0 e^{-t/\tau}$ where $I_0 = \dfrac{V_0}{R} = \dfrac{500}{100} = 5 \, \text{A}$

The graph shows exponential decay from $I = 5 \, \text{A}$ at $t = 0$ toward $I = 0$ as $t \to \infty$.

After one time constant (1.2 ms), current drops to $I_0/e \approx 1.84 \, \text{A}$.

‚ö†Ô∏è Nuance: For discharge, current direction is opposite to charging. The initial current $I_0 = V_0/R$ can be quite large (here 5A) because the capacitor voltage is high initially. Notice that after one time constant, the charge drops to about 37% of initial (not 63% like in charging).

Common Patterns

Capacitance Formulas:

- Parallel-plate (air): $C = \varepsilon_0 A/d$ - Parallel-plate (dielectric): $C = k\varepsilon_0 A/d$ - Series combination: $1/C_{eq} = \sum 1/C_i$ - Parallel combination: $C_{eq} = \sum C_i$

Energy Stored:

- $U = \frac{1}{2}CV^2 = \frac{Q^2}{2C} = \frac{1}{2}QV$

RC Circuits - Charging:

- $Q(t) = Q_0(1 - e^{-t/\tau})$ - $I(t) = \frac{V_0}{R}e^{-t/\tau}$ - $\tau = RC$ (time constant)

RC Circuits - Discharging:

- $Q(t) = Q_0 e^{-t/\tau}$ - $I(t) = -\frac{Q_0}{RC} e^{-t/\tau} = -\frac{V_0}{R}e^{-t/\tau}$ - At $t = \tau$: charge drops to $36.8\%$ of initial

Key Percentages:

- After 1œÑ: 63.2% charged, 36.8% discharged - After 2œÑ: 86.5% charged, 13.5% discharged - After 3œÑ: 95.0% charged, 5.0% discharged - After 5œÑ: 99.3% charged, 0.7% discharged
